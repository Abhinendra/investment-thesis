# Indian New-Energy Stock Scorecard

Date: 2026-05-31

This is a provisional research scorecard for Indian listed new-energy stocks after excluding EV charging, power electronics, and wind-focused players. It is not a buy/sell recommendation. Scores should be refreshed with latest annual reports, investor presentations, cash-flow statements, valuation data, and management commentary before taking investment decisions.

## Investor Profile And Time Horizon

The base investment horizon is 3 to 5 years, with willingness to stay invested beyond that if the company keeps executing and the original thesis improves or remains intact. The approach is not a short-term trade. It is a staged ownership plan where position size can be built gradually over months or years using salary-linked savings.

Quarterly review should focus on whether the thesis is intact, improving, or broken. Exit decisions should be based on fundamental thesis change, not only stock price movement.

## Position-Building Framework

Because new-energy themes can be volatile and valuations can remain stretched for long periods, position building should be staggered.

Suggested structure:

- Start with a tracking position only after business quality, valuation, and key risks are understood.
- Add gradually over several months or quarters when execution confirms the thesis.
- Increase allocation only when both business progress and valuation are acceptable.
- Avoid building full position immediately in loss-making or high-capex companies.
- Keep a separate watchlist for companies that are high quality but too expensive.

Possible allocation buckets:

| Bucket | Company Type | Position Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Core compounder candidate | Strong returns, credible moat, manageable risk | Build gradually and hold through cycles if thesis remains intact |
| Transition compounder | Legacy cash flows funding new-energy shift | Smaller allocation until new-energy returns are proven |
| Optionality bet | Loss-making or high-execution-risk but large upside | Small staged position only; add after milestones are met |
| Watchlist only | Good theme but weak financials, governance concern, or stretched valuation | Track quarterly; no rush to buy |

## Quarterly Thesis Review

Every quarter, classify each company into one of four states:

| Status | Meaning | Action Bias |
|---|---|---|
| Thesis improving | Revenue, margins, cash flows, order book, or competitive position improving faster than expected | Consider adding if valuation is reasonable |
| Thesis intact | Progress broadly matches expectations | Hold or add slowly |
| Thesis weakening | One or two key assumptions deteriorate, but not decisively broken | Pause additions and monitor next quarter |
| Thesis broken | Core assumption fails: governance issue, structural margin collapse, loss of market share, debt stress, failed capex, or policy reversal | Reassess exit |

Key quarterly questions:

- Is revenue growth coming with margin discipline?
- Are receivables, inventory, and debt under control?
- Is operating cash flow improving or worsening?
- Is management delivering on stated capacity, product, or project milestones?
- Is market share improving without irrational discounting?
- Is the company becoming more competitive without depending only on subsidies or protection?
- Has valuation become disconnected from realistic execution?

## Scoring Weights

Ratings are on a 10-point scale. Weighted score is out of 100.

| Factor | Weight | What It Measures |
|---|---:|---|
| Management / execution / governance | 20% | Execution, disclosure quality, capital allocation, governance comfort |
| Moat / right to win | 20% | Scale, technology, cost position, brand, supply chain, customer access |
| Government support / localization push | 15% | Benefit from PLI, ALMM, domestic content rules, import duties, renewable targets, EV policies, and storage policies |
| Growth opportunity | 15% | Size and durability of sector tailwind |
| Geopolitical / supply-chain resilience | 10% | China+1 benefit, US export access, upstream dependence, tariff risk, technology and equipment dependence |
| Free cash flow quality | 7.5% | Cash conversion after working capital and capex |
| ROCE / ROE | 7.5% | Capital efficiency and ability to earn attractive returns |
| Risk adjustment | 5% | Balance-sheet risk, execution risk, competition, valuation, regulatory or service risk |

For this sector, management and government support deserve higher weight than current ROCE/ROE because many companies are still in investment, scale-up, or policy-driven industry formation phases. Current return ratios still matter, but they should be used as discipline checks rather than the primary ranking driver.

## Geopolitical And Localization Lens

China and US policy risks are material in this sector. Indian companies can benefit from China+1 sourcing, US tariffs on Chinese supply chains, Indian localization policies, and domestic manufacturing incentives. At the same time, many companies remain dependent on Chinese wafers, cells, lithium, graphite, separators, equipment, or low-cost components.

What to track:

- Solar: Chinese oversupply, US import scrutiny, domestic cell/module rules, wafer and polysilicon dependence.
- EV: Chinese battery/component cost advantage, local cell manufacturing, product localization, and subsidy changes.
- BESS: cell sourcing, local pack/container integration, grid storage incentives, and technology dependence.
- Renewable IPPs: module and BESS cost deflation helps project economics, but sudden import restrictions can disrupt execution.

## May 2026 Results And Margin Refresh

Latest May/FY26 updates change the emphasis in three areas: localization, margin trajectory, and whether scale is converting into better unit economics.

Key updates:

- Waaree Energies delivered very strong FY26 growth with revenue around Rs 26,537 crore, EBITDA around Rs 5,909 crore, and PAT around Rs 3,884 crore. Localization progress is meaningful: cell capacity is scaling, US module capacity is moving toward 4.2 GW, and the company is investing across ingot-wafer, PV glass, batteries, inverters, and other energy-transition adjacencies. Margin pressure remains from silver, copper, logistics, lower overseas mix, and external cell procurement.
- Premier Energies delivered FY26 total income around Rs 8,026 crore, PAT around Rs 1,510 crore, and EBITDA margin around 32.1%. Q4 EBITDA margin compressed to about 30.3%, mainly due to raw material costs and mix, but the June 2026 ALMM List-II cell mandate materially improves the localization story for domestic integrated cell/module manufacturers.
- Ather Energy showed one of the clearest margin improvements among EV names. FY26 adjusted gross margin improved to about 24%, Q4 adjusted gross margin reached about 25%, and EBITDA margin improved to around negative 2.5% in Q4. Management also claims about 99% domestic component sourcing excluding lithium-ion cells, with 80% hardware and 100% software developed in-house.
- Ola Electric's Q4 was operationally weak on revenue and volumes, but the margin/cash-flow story improved. Q4 gross margin reached about 38.5%, or about 33.5% excluding PLI, and the company reported its first positive operating cash-flow quarter. The cell story is improving, with 2.5 GWh operational capacity, 6 GWh installation largely complete, and a plan to scale toward 20 GWh through a separate cell-entity capital raise. The risk is that volume recovery and service consistency still need proof.
- Amara Raja remains financially stable, but the new-energy transition is still early. FY26 consolidated EBITDA margin narrowed to about 10.8% from 12.6%, with margin pressure from raw materials and new-energy ramp costs. New energy revenue grew around 60%, but it is still a small part of revenue. BESS is becoming more important, with initial BESS margins guided around 6-7% and potential improvement at scale.
- NTPC Green Energy showed strong revenue growth but margin pressure. Q4 revenue grew nearly 40% YoY, but profit fell YoY and operating margin compressed due to depreciation, interest, and expansion costs. The BESS push is getting larger, including an 800 MW / 3,200 MWh tender in Gujarat.
- ACME Solar delivered a strong margin story with FY26 EBITDA margin around 90.3%, PAT margin around 19.9%, and major BESS progress. The company has commissioned about 2.3 GWh of BESS and has a large under-construction BESS pipeline. Localization is weaker than manufacturers because ACME relies on module availability and imports BESS packs from global Tier-1 suppliers for warranty and lender reliability.

## Company Notes

### Waaree Energies

Strongest solar manufacturing scale candidate. The right to win comes from module scale, export presence, large order book, domestic manufacturing tailwinds, and vertical integration plans. China+1 and Indian localization policies are positives, and the US manufacturing footprint reduces some tariff exposure. Latest numbers show strong revenue, EBITDA, and PAT growth, but Q4 margins were pressured by silver, copper, logistics, lower overseas mix, and external cell procurement. Main concern is free cash flow quality: profits are strong, but working capital and capex have absorbed significant cash.

Key questions:

- Can working capital normalize as capacity scales?
- Can the company protect margins if global module prices remain under pressure?
- How much of growth depends on policy protection versus durable cost competitiveness?
- Does US capacity expansion continue to reduce tariff and China-linked supply-chain risk?

### Premier Energies

Clean solar cell and module manufacturing play with very strong ROE/ROCE and high margins. It benefits from domestic cell requirements, ALMM-style localization, and possible industry consolidation as policy moves deeper into domestic value addition. The June 2026 ALMM List-II enforcement is a meaningful positive for domestic cell manufacturers. Q4 margin compression needs monitoring, but the broader margin level remains strong. The key geopolitical question is whether it can reduce upstream dependence and stay competitive if Chinese pricing pressure persists.

Key questions:

- Are current margins structurally sustainable or partly helped by timing and policy?
- Can capex be executed without hurting returns?
- Does Premier build enough upstream capability to defend against global competition?
- Does ALMM List-II translate into higher utilization without excessive margin give-up?

### Amara Raja Energy & Mobility

Best financial stability in this group because the legacy battery business generates cash and the balance sheet is strong. The new-energy opportunity is meaningful through lithium packs, BESS, and cell manufacturing. New energy revenue grew strongly, but it is still a small share of revenue and currently drags margins. BESS is becoming a larger part of the strategy, with initial operating margins guided around 6-7% and upside possible at scale.

The lithium cell IP story is weaker than a fully owned technology platform. Amara Raja's LFP cell plan is based on a technical licensing agreement with GIB EnergyX Slovakia, linked to Gotion High-Tech through Gotion-InoBat. This gives faster access to proven LFP cell technology, IP support, process support, and supply-chain integration, but it also creates China-linked technology and supply-chain dependence. The company remains a transition story rather than a pure play.

Key questions:

- Can the new-energy business scale to meaningful profit contribution?
- Can lithium/BESS earn acceptable ROCE after heavy capex?
- Does the lead-acid business remain resilient enough to fund the transition?
- Can BESS margins move from early 6-7% levels toward attractive double-digit returns?
- Can Amara Raja reduce dependence on licensed/Gotion-linked technology over time?

### NTPC Green Energy

Strong government alignment, parentage, project pipeline, and access to capital. It benefits heavily from India's renewable targets and state-backed energy transition. Latest results show strong revenue growth, but profit and margins are pressured by depreciation, interest, and project commissioning costs. The BESS opportunity is getting larger through major tenders, including large Gujarat storage capacity. Geopolitical risk is less about exports and more about module/BESS sourcing, project costs, and sudden changes in import/localization rules. It is a clean listed renewable platform, but the financial profile is capital-intensive with low ROE/ROCE during expansion.

Key questions:

- Can incremental projects earn attractive returns after debt costs?
- Does scale convert into better capital efficiency?
- Will valuation stay reasonable relative to low near-term returns?
- Can BESS projects improve blended return profile rather than only increase capex?

### ACME Solar

Focused renewable IPP with solar, hybrid, and BESS/FDRE exposure. Growth visibility is strong through contracted capacity. Latest numbers show a strong IPP margin story, with very high EBITDA margin, improving PAT, and meaningful BESS progress. Lower global module/BESS costs can improve project economics, but import restrictions or supply shocks can hurt execution. Localization is weaker than manufacturers because BESS packs are imported from global Tier-1 suppliers for warranty and lender reliability. The model is debt-heavy and free cash flow is likely to remain negative during expansion.

Key questions:

- Can ACME keep financing costs under control?
- Can BESS/FDRE projects improve returns versus plain solar IPP assets?
- How resilient are cash flows to execution delays and counterparty risk?
- Does imported BESS reliance remain acceptable if India tightens localization rules?

### Ather Energy

Higher-quality EV product and brand perception, improving gross margins, rising market share, and better unit economics. The May/FY26 update materially improves the margin story: adjusted gross margin rose to about 24% for FY26 and around 25% in Q4, while Q4 EBITDA margin improved sharply. Localization also looks better than initially captured, with management indicating about 99% domestic component sourcing excluding lithium-ion cells. It benefits from EV localization policy, but remains exposed to battery cells and some component supply chains where China has scale advantages. Still, it is not yet financially proven because losses and negative free cash flow remain.

Key questions:

- Can Ather reach EBITDA and FCF breakeven without slowing growth?
- Can it defend premium positioning against Ola, TVS, Bajaj, Hero, and others?
- Does non-vehicle revenue become a real moat or remain a small add-on?
- Can local cell sourcing and LFP transition improve margins without hurting product quality?

### Ola Electric

Purest listed EV scale play with high optionality from EV two-wheelers, vertical integration, cells, and software/services. Government localization and cell manufacturing policies are central to the upside case. The May/FY26 update improves the margin and cash-flow story: Q4 gross margin was strong, including a healthy margin even excluding PLI, and the company reported its first positive operating cash-flow quarter. However, Q4 revenue and volumes were weak, and the cell expansion roadmap has changed multiple times. The risk profile is high because market share, service quality, product reliability, cell execution, imported component dependence, and cash burn need consistent improvement.

Key questions:

- Can Ola regain volume momentum without sacrificing margins?
- Can service and product quality issues be sustainably fixed?
- Can the cell/gigafactory ramp create cost advantage rather than additional execution risk?
- Can the company sustain Q4 margin gains when volumes recover?

## Valuation Framework For New-Energy Stocks

Plain P/E and P/B are weak primary tools here because many companies are in capex, scale-up, subsidy, or loss-making phases. Use operating and capacity-linked valuation instead.

### Solar Manufacturers

Useful metrics:

- Market cap or EV / next-year EBITDA
- Market cap / order book
- EV / effective GW of module and cell capacity
- EBITDA margin sustainability
- Operating cash flow / PAT
- Free cash flow trend after capex
- Revenue visibility from order book and retail/distributed channel

Reasonable valuation bands:

| Metric | Attractive | Fair | Stretched |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| EV / next-year EBITDA | Below 10x | 10x-14x | Above 16x |
| Market cap / order book | Below 1.5x | 1.5x-2.5x | Above 3x |
| OCF / PAT | Above 70% | 40%-70% | Below 40% |
| FCF trend | Improving or near breakeven | Negative but explainable by high-return capex | Persistently negative with rising debt |

Interpretation:

- Waaree looks reasonable only if FY27 EBITDA guidance is credible and working capital normalizes. Recent market cap around Rs 90,000 crore versus FY27 EBITDA guidance of Rs 7,000-7,700 crore implies roughly low-teens EBITDA multiple, which is acceptable for a scale leader if cash conversion improves.
- Premier's valuation depends heavily on market cap used. Around Rs 32,000 crore it looks reasonable relative to FY26 EBITDA of about Rs 2,579 crore and strong FY27 cell ramp. Around Rs 45,000 crore it becomes much more demanding and needs flawless margin execution.

### EV OEMs

Useful metrics:

- EV / forward revenue
- EV / adjusted gross profit
- Market cap / annual vehicles sold
- Gross margin excluding subsidies
- EBITDA breakeven volume
- Cash burn versus cash balance
- Market-share trend without discounting
- Service quality and warranty risk

Reasonable valuation bands:

| Metric | Attractive | Fair | Stretched |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| EV / forward revenue | Below 3x | 3x-5x | Above 6x |
| EV / adjusted gross profit | Below 12x | 12x-20x | Above 25x |
| EBITDA breakeven visibility | Within 4 quarters | Within 6-8 quarters | Unclear |
| Gross margin ex-subsidy | Above 25% | 18%-25% | Below 18% |

Interpretation:

- Ola is not low risk, but valuation is less stretched after the correction. Market cap around Rs 12,000 crore against FY26 revenue of about Rs 2,253 crore and improving gross margin makes it a high-upside, execution-led bet. It deserves allocation only if volume recovery and service quality improve.
- Ather has cleaner execution and product quality, but valuation is demanding. Market cap around Rs 35,000-37,000 crore against FY26 revenue of about Rs 3,672 crore and adjusted gross profit around Rs 925 crore implies a high multiple of current gross profit. It should be bought slowly and preferably on drawdowns, despite better execution.

### Renewable IPP / BESS Players

Useful metrics:

- EV / EBITDA
- EV / operational MW
- EV / contracted MW
- EV / BESS GWh, only if revenue visibility is clear
- Net debt / EBITDA
- PPA-signed capacity percentage
- DSO and receivable days
- Project IRR versus borrowing cost

Reasonable valuation bands:

| Metric | Attractive | Fair | Stretched |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| EV / EBITDA | Below 10x | 10x-15x | Above 18x |
| Net debt / EBITDA | Below 4x | 4x-6x | Above 6x |
| PPA-backed portfolio | Above 80% | 60%-80% | Below 60% |
| DSO trend | Improving | Stable | Worsening |

Interpretation:

- ACME is richer than a plain utility, but the BESS/FDRE execution story partly justifies a premium. EV/EBITDA around high-teens is not cheap, so entry discipline matters.
- NTPC Green has strong strategic value but current valuation appears stretched relative to present EBITDA and returns. It should be a small position or watchlist until either price corrects or earnings/capacity catch up.

## Staged Deployment Plan

For a salaried investor building over months or years, use tranches rather than one-time deployment.

| Stage | Deploy | Trigger |
|---|---:|---|
| Initial tracking allocation | 25% of target | Only after valuation check and comfort with thesis |
| Execution confirmation | 25% of target | Next 1-2 quarters confirm margins, order book, capacity ramp, or market share |
| Valuation or drawdown add | 25% of target | Price corrects while thesis remains intact |
| Conviction add | 25% of target | Company proves cash flow, localization, or scale economics |

Position sizing rules:

- Do not fully allocate to loss-making EV names until EBITDA and cash-flow milestones improve.
- Do not add to solar manufacturers only because revenue grows; check margins, receivables, inventory, and capex.
- Do not add to IPPs only because capacity grows; check debt cost, project IRR, and cash conversion.
- Treat valuation as a gating factor before actual deployment.
